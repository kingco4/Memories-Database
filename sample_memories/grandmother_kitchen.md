---
title: Grandmother's Kitchen in Winter
type: journal
date: 2019-12-27
tags: nostalgia, family, childhood, warmth, loss
---

It is only in winter that I can smell it properly — her kitchen. Bay leaves and something like dust and something like bread and the particular warmth of a gas stove left on all day. This is the smell of every Christmas I can remember until I was twelve.

She kept a painted rooster on the windowsill above the sink. Red comb, proud chest, slightly chipped at the base. I don't know where it is now. None of us thought to save it.

She taught me how to roll pasta by hand. My palms too small, my pressure uneven, the dough tearing constantly. She never took it from me. She just guided my wrists with her hands, patient in a way I understood was rare even then.

I wonder if she knew she was becoming a memory while she was still alive. That these moments — the rooster, the dough, the December light — would calcify into something I'd carry for the rest of my life, untouchable.

Tonight I tried to make her sauce from memory. I don't have the recipe. I don't think there was one. I stood over the pot and kept adding things, kept tasting, kept failing to close the distance between now and then.

It wasn't bad. It just wasn't hers. I ate it standing at the counter, alone, which felt right.
